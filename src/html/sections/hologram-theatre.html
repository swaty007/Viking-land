1. Birka Warrior

Elite warriors served as guards in the merchant town of Birka in Sweden. The armour he is wearing is composed of small, thin iron platelets, called lamellae, laced together with straps. This created a flexible, partly telescope-like tube around the torso and provided better protection than mail armour. Beneath his armour, he is protected by a leather gambeson. He carries the traditional long knife as a weapon, which was common among Vikings in the east, and a helmet inspired by finds in Gotland and other sites.

3. BLACKSMITH

The blacksmith was one of the most essential craftsmen during the Viking Age. He produced iron weapons, tools and utensils. The blacksmith wore a simple, plant-dyed linen kirtle and a leather apron. Wide-legged trousers were common during the Viking Age, and were used all over Scandinavia during this period. This assumption is based on various images from jewellery and textiles. The shoes are inspired by finds from the Oseberg burial mound in Vestfold, Norway.

4. Mistress of modest stature

The mistress of the house was a key figure in Norse culture, which focused on rural farming culture. Women were bearers of traditions and practical knowledge. The mistress of the house wears a linen "serk" (underdress), with a aprondress in diamond twill wool fabric. Her oval brooches are from finds in Hof in Vestfold, and date from the 800s. Her bead necklace is based on finds from the Oseberg mound. The knife, bone needle case and scissors are common accessories and hang from the brooches. Her head covering is inferred, based on a description of the "sveigr", a long piece of fabric a woman wound around her head. The mistress holds a whalebone plaque, which was perhaps used in sewing as a surface for smoothing out seams and for ironing smaller pieces of fabric. This is based on finds from Norway.

5. Slave

Vikings took numerous slaves, both at home in Scandinavia and in the regions they visited. Slaves made up a large part of the work force, and at most, they comprised about 30% of the Scandinavian population. This slave is wearing a simple outfit made of natural linen and an iron collar, based on finds from the merchant town Birka in Sweden. The slave’s primary tasks involved working on the farm and caring for livestock. Some slaves were given trusted positions. Slaves could potentially win their freedom by showing courage and loyalty to their masters.

6. The Oseberg Queen

Among the finds of the famous Oseberg mound were the remains of a woman of high stature. The remnants of her garments indicate that she most likely wore a hand-sewn dress of diamond twill, madder-dyed red. The silk embroidery is a copy of that found in the burial mound. Silk was appliqued in ribbons. The cut was inspired by French fashion at the time, copied by the upper classes. The dress also has a train, as found depicted on several pieces of jewellery from this period.

7. Farmer of
modest stature

Farmers were the backbone of Norse society. The farmer wears Thorsberg trousers of brown wool, a linen shirt with goresand a sealskin cap. He bears a bronze amulet in the shape of Thor's hammer around his neck to protect him on his journeys. On his back is a backpack based upon a find from the Gokstad mound in Vestfold. The top and bottom are designed according to original measurements, and the sides are made from birch bark.

8. Merchant mistress from Kaupang

Merchant towns arose throughout the Nordic regions during the Viking Age. These were populated by merchants and craftsmen under the protection of powerful nobles. The merchant mistress from Kaupang in Vestfold wears a short, lined, woollen coat of diamond twill, with inlaid silk thread over a wool aprondress. Her clothes are decorated with various types of tabletwoven bands from various finds from this period. She wears a headband of tablet-woven silk and shoes based on finds from the Oseberg burial mound in Vestfold.

9. High-ranking
trader from Birka

Birka in Sweden was a merchant town and centre of trade for the entire Baltic region, extending far into what is now Russia. Elite tradesmen here became very wealthy. The trader wears an Eastern-inspired outfit.The kaftan is of plant-dyed green wool with bands of linen, decorated with a woven band of silver brocade, based on finds from the merchant town of Birka in Sweden. The cap is trimmed with wolf fur, with a silver tip and buttons based on finds from the same burial mound. The shoes are expertly made, and are based on finds from Staraja Ladoga in Russia. The long knife is based on a find from Birka. The linen kirtle has a block printedapplique, resembling Byzantine fashion. This is based on a find from Germany, from the late 1000s.

10. Mistress from Dublin

Dublin in Ireland was the most important Viking city outside Scandinavia, and a centre for the trade of slaves, wine and luxury items from the rest of the known world. The mistress of the house is a younger woman from a higher social class in the merchant city. Her aprondress is of hand-woven wool, based on finds from Hedeby, Denmark, with a pleated hemp and silk "serk" or underdress. Her brooches are made of gold-plated bronze, according to finds from Lisbjerg Skov north of Århus, Denmark. Her bonnet is of white silk based on finds in Dublin. She holds a set of bone weaving tablets, decorated with etched circles. These were used to weave the brocaded tabletwoven bands with gold thread decorating the front of the dress.

11. Viking chieftain
from Novgorod

Norse chieftains settled along the Russian rivers and controlled trade and passage. This made them very wealthy and powerful. The chieftain wears a kaftan of plant-dyed, hand spun and hand-woven wool, decorated with bands of yellow silk. Strips of fabric with Byzantine silk print are used for decoration around the cuffs and the lower hem. The kaftan is lined with dyed linen. Beneath the kaftan, he wears a tunic of plant-dyed wool adorned with embroidered linen at the neck and cuffs. The chieftain wears traditional wide-legged trousers in linen, and a cap with leather trim. His long knife was pattern welded, and has a walrus tooth handle.

12. Farmwoman
of low stature

Smallholding farmers were free, but not wealthy in terms of goods. Nevertheless, they did what they could to copy the elite in their use of jewellery and colour, only of poorer quality. The peasant woman wears a naturally white “serk” or underdress in linen and a simple herringbonepattern aprondress of woad-dyed wool. She wears simple leather shoes and needle-bound socks. The oval broochesare bronze, but not gilded or ornamental, with a simple bead necklace hanging between them. The peasant woman carries a basket of wool and a spinning wheel.

13. Warrior from
the Danelaw

The Danelaw was a region in England where Viking law ruled for a long period, and where settlers from the Great Army settled during the 800s. The Danelaw Viking wears a woollen wrap-around warrior coat, an image of which was displayed on several pieces of jewellery from the Viking Age and in the period prior to this. Beneath the coat, he wears a blue woollen shirt with silk print on the chest, and an undershirt in yellow linen. He wears a belt with a brass buckle from Gotland, a knife with a whale tooth handle, woollen Thorsberg trousers, needle-bound socks and leg wrappings. The shoes are based on a find from Hedeby. His favourite weapon is the Dane Axe. He wears makeup as described in a 10th century account from Hedeby in Denmark.

14. High-ranking
mistress from Birka

The merchant town of Birka, in Sweden, had a very wealthy upper class that had broad access to exclusive clothing and objects. The mistress of the house wears a dress inspired by a burial mound find that included two people, a man and a woman. Finds of brocaded tablet-woven bands with silver indicates that this was a sumptuous dress. The wool apron dress has a silk front piece beneath the woven bands, and is pulled over a "serk" or underdress of linen and a long-sleeved dress of red wool twill. Between the oval broochesof gold-plated bronze is a chain holding a silver needle case, among other things. The necklace is made of carnelian and rock crystal, which were popular gems during the Viking Age. Her hairstyle and cloak of wool and silk are inspired by a female figure found in Tuna in Sweden.

15. Hunter

Hunting and trapping animals for food was one of the most important tasks in Norse society. The hunter’s garments have been inferred based on the garments of Northern Norwegian reindeer and walrus hunters. The leather kirtleis based on traditional Northern Norwegian rain gear for use at sea. These were waterproofed with a blend of wood tar and cod liver oil, and are both windproof and waterproof. The hood is based on a hood from the Norwegian Skjoldehamn finds. The trousers are Thorsberg wadmal. The longbow is made from yew, and is based on a find from Ballinderry in Ireland. The arrows are copies of a glacier find in Norway. A simple hunting knife hangs from the belt. The shoes are based on a find from York, England.

16. The Gjermundbu Chieftain

One of the most spectacular warrior burial mounds in Norway is the Gjermundbu mound in Ringerike. A chieftain was buried here with his helmet and chainmailarmour in the mid-900s. The Gjermundbu chieftain wore a mail armour consisting of about 15,000 rings, where every other ring was riveted and punched. Beneath this he wears thick leather pauldrons to absorb stabs and cuts from enemy weapons. On his head he wears a riveted “spectacle” helmet, but with a chainmailhood to protect the head and neck. He wears Thorsberg trousers and a lined woollen cloak in red madder-dyed, diamond twill, decorated with yellow tablet-woven bands. He has a Mammen style pattern weldedsword, found in Egge in Trøndelag.

17. Volva

The volve, or female shaman, was a highly respected and feared religious figure. The volvewomen were paid to perform various rituals to heal the sick, assist in childbirth or acquire knowledge of what the gods and higher powers wanted. This volve wears a woollen dress in natural brown wool. She also wears a cloak of blue wool with silk edges. This is freely inferred from a description by Erik the Red’s Saga. It is fastened with a cloak brooch clasp based on finds from Birka. In her hands, she holds a “volvestaff”, which is an iron staff with bronze ornamentation. Such staffs have been found in several larger excavations of female burial mounds, and are considered symbols of the women’s status as ritual leaders.

18. Peasant warrior

A peasant had both a right and a duty to bear arms such as the spear, axe and shield. In times of war, farmers made up the core of the country’s defence. He wears a gambeson of plant-dyed wool, red madder-dyed woollen Thorsberg trousers and yellow onion-dyed leg wrappings of wool. He wears a waterproof Skjoldhamn type leatherhood. A bone comb hangs from his belt. This is based on finds from Løken in Eidsvoll. He has a York type belt, but the tip and buckle are made of ornamental bone. The farmer warrior is armed with a simple axe and a simple round shield.

19. Farmer’s wife
with child

Responsibility for child rearing and the duty to continue the family line was an essential part of the peasant woman’s life. She wears a simple wool strap dress, based on finds from Hedeby from the 900 – 1000s, over a “serk” of thin, bleached wool. Her shoes are based on finds from York in England, and she has a simple pair of bronze oval brooches, decorated with animal figures. The child wears a linen kirtle.

20. Skald

Skalds were the most sought-after entertainers for Viking chieftain halls. They often had permanent positions with a chieftain or king and wrote poetry in skaldic verse about their deeds. They also told mystical tales and stories with morals. The skald wears a blue, woad-dyed woollen kaftan over a reddish madder-dyed Skjoldhamn shirt. He carries an Eastern long knife, Thorsberg trousers and shoes resembling those from Oseberg. A wolfskin is draped across his shoulders to indicate that he belongs to the cult worshipping the god Odin. This was a group that had close ties to the most powerful chieftain in the country.

21A. High-ranking mistress

The mistress of the house was an important figure in Norse culture, which focused on rural farming culture. Upper class women were also bearers of traditions and practical knowledge. The mistress of the house often had a role in political alliances. The mistress wears a madder-dyed red "Birka type" dress. Her strap dress is of woad-dyed wool decorated with a silk and brocade tabletwovenband, based on finds from Birka, dating from 900s. The oval brooches, collar brooch, pin case and knife are all based on finds from Birka in Sweden.

21B. Unmarried woman
from Kaupang

Young unmarried women from the city communities may have had better opportunities for personal development than women in the rural villages. Music and singing skills were sought after, and in the city communities and chieftain halls, musicians could provide entertainment and create an atmosphere. She wears a naturally white handsewn linen “serk” with a simple strap dress and tabletwovenband from Vestrum in Vestfold. She has a long, woad-dyed, blue coat and shoes, based on finds from Hedeby in Denmark. Since she is unmarried, she does not wear oval brooches. The lyre is inspired by finds from periods prior to and following the Viking Age.

21C. Lyre player

Musicians and skalds were sought-after entertainers in Viking chieftain halls. They often travelled around and did service for various masters for brief periods. The lyre skald wears fairly valuable garments. He wears madder-dyed Thorsberg trousers of herringbonewool and a thick madder-dyed wool tunic with blue fabric decorating the neck, cuffs and lower hem. His shoes are based on finds from York in England. He plays the lyre.

21E. Drummer

Musicians and skalds were sought-after entertainers. Many were able to play different instruments to a certain extent, and drums were used both in religious ceremonies and as entertainment. The drummer wears a naturally white "serk" in linen undera woad-dyed blue coat, held together with a trefoil brooch. She wears simple leather shoes and needle-bound socks.
